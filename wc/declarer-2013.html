Declarer leaderboard — four facets, 2013 World Championships

Every declarer with ≥40 boards in each division, ranked by F4 — trick-taking scored in IMPs against the field in the identical contract on the same deal. The lighter facets show the same skill through different lenses; ranking uses the stakes-weighted one.

Source Bali 2013 · RR + KO · peers = other tables in the same strain+level+side on that deal · leave-one-out

F1 · tricks vs DD

Actual tricks − double-dummy. The single-reference measure — useful, but DD is one fixed yardstick, not the field.

F2 · tricks vs field

Actual tricks − average taken by other declarers in the same contract on the deal. Pure card-play in tricks.

F3 · making vs field

Did you make it − the field's make-rate for that contract on the deal. Positive = brings home contracts peers fail.

F4 · IMPs vs field

The ranking. F2's trick edge scored in IMPs — a trick that swings a game counts for what it's worth. Field-relative and stakes-aware.

Peer matching

Default compares only tables where the same seat declared (2♥-E vs 2♥-E) — same hand concealed, same opening leader. Toggle to either seat (the looser side match) to see the difference. F1 doesn't depend on this.

F1/F2 are trick-count facets; F4 restores stakes by scoring the same-contract trick difference in IMPs — a player can be flat on F1/F2 yet stand out on F4.

Why four. F1 leans on double-dummy; F2–F4 lean on the field, which is the fairer bar. F2/F3 are in natural units (tricks, make-rate) and ignore stakes; F4 restores stakes by scoring the same-contract trick difference in IMPs. A player can be flat on F1/F2 yet high on F4 if their tricks land on the boards that matter. Declaring only; defence and bidding are separate. Everything is vs the full division field, so a positive number means better than a typical table at the championship.
